\begin{abstract}
Deploying deep learning for chest X-ray pneumonia classification on third-party cloud infrastructure exposes raw patient imagery to membership inference and model-inversion attacks. We present a split-model architecture in which a hospital-side three-convolution encoder produces 128-dimensional embeddings consumed by a lightweight cloud-side classification head, and systematically evaluate three privacy-preserving transformations---Differential Privacy (DP) with Laplace noise on embeddings, Block-wise Image Encryption (BIE) on input images, and Secure Multi-Party Computation (SMPC) via additive secret sharing---across all eight combinations on the Kaggle chest X-ray pneumonia dataset. Each configuration is attacked with (i) Yeom threshold and Shokri shadow-model membership inference attacks and (ii) a decoder-based reconstruction attack. Our central finding is a threat-model mismatch: image-level (BIE) and transit-level (SMPC) defenses leave output confidences unchanged and consequently provide no membership inference protection, while embedding-level DP reduces reconstruction PSNR by 3.2 dB with only a 1.7-point utility loss. DP is both necessary and sufficient for reconstruction defense in this regime; BIE and SMPC must be composed with output-level privacy to be meaningful against inference-time adversaries.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
The transition to an AI-integrated healthcare environment marks a significant break from legacy data governance strategies. This shift replaces traditional, manual oversight with autonomous systems capable of navigating the complex intersection of data accessibility and patient confidentiality \cite{b1}. Unlike older frameworks that often relied on human controlled protocols, which frequently created operational bottlenecks, modern medical environments are turning to intelligent platforms that dynamically classify sensitive information and adjust security measures based on real-time context. This evolution is important for maintaining regulatory compliance without giving up the speed or quality of medical services \cite{b1}.

The reliance on cloud-based deep learning for medical imaging, particularly for chest X-ray analysis, introduces a vulnerability to the exposure of raw, personally identifiable patient data to third-party servers. Even though centralized models offer high diagnostic accuracy, the legal and ethical risks associated with data breaches have caused a need for Privacy-Preserving Machine Learning (PPML) solutions that decouple diagnostic utility from data visibility.

In this work we (i) implement a split-model chest X-ray pneumonia classifier in which raw images never leave the hospital, (ii) compose three mainstream PPML transformations---Differential Privacy, Block-wise Image Encryption, and Secure Multi-Party Computation---into all eight possible combinations, and (iii) measure the residual privacy leakage of each configuration under two membership inference attacks and one reconstruction attack. Our evaluation highlights that each defense targets a specific adversarial surface, and that combining them naively does not confer cumulative protection. Rather, the layer at which the defense operates determines which attack families it can influence at all.

\section{Literature Review}
The rapid evolution of deep learning has made medical imaging a cornerstone of modern diagnostics, with current applications ranging from automated tumor detection to complex segmentation of pathological structures. These advancements in medical imaging and data transmission introduced privacy risks, prompting the development of encryption algorithms that can handle the high redundancy inherent in imaging data \cite{panzade}.

The traditional approach to AI, in which massive amounts of patient data are funneled into a single cloud server, creates a significant legal and ethical bottleneck. Hospitals are currently caught between the need for high-performance cloud-based diagnostics and the strict mandates of HIPAA and GDPR, which forbid the sharing of unencrypted, personally identifiable data. This tension caused the rise of Privacy-Preserving Machine Learning (PPML), a field dedicated to developing models that analyze the disease without revealing patient information. The mechanisms of PPML are systematically categorized into four primary pillars \cite{panzade}.

Federated Learning (FL) is a decentralized training paradigm in which medical institutions collaboratively train global models by exchanging only local model updates, such as weights or gradients, rather than raw patient images. This ensures that sensitive data remains behind local firewalls, reducing the risks associated with central data storage. Something that complements this is Differential Privacy (DP), which provides a statistical guarantee of privacy by injecting controlled noise into datasets or model parameters. This mechanism ensures that the presence of any single patient's record does not significantly alter the model's output, which prevents re-identification or membership inference attacks \cite{panzade}.

In addition to decentralization and statistical noise, cryptographic techniques provide even greater privacy by performing computations with raw data hidden from the host system. These include homomorphic encryption (HE), which allows performing math on encrypted data in a way that even the model cannot obtain any information from the image. Likewise, secure multi-party computation (SMPC) allows performing computations in the same way, except that the data is split into different parts (``shares'') that can be used to reconstruct the original object once decrypted. SMPC has been extensively discussed as an encryption-based PPML solution, with a variety of frameworks available for training machine learning algorithms while distributing sensitive data across non-colluding servers \cite{secureml}. For instance, SecureML can train both linear and neural network models using data represented as secret shares and perform computations using secure arithmetic operations. Although SMPC provides much better privacy than previous MPC-based models and significantly improves scalability, it introduces new computational and communication costs, thereby limiting its practicality for medical imaging applications.

Another innovation in the field is introduced by the Swarm Learning technique which allows fully decentralized training without a central orchestrator by integrating PPML with blockchain and peer-to-peer networking techniques \cite{panzade}.

Finally, while there is a range of PPML techniques for ML data, Panzade et al. (2024) highlight the critical ``privacy-utility trade-off'' involved in implementing PPML technologies \cite{panzade}. In addition to membership inference attacks described above, model inversion attacks represent another major threat. Specifically, as demonstrated by Fredrikson et al., an adversary can perform model inversion by utilizing access to the model's output to extract features from sensitive input data \cite{fredrikson}. Their research reveals that even the black-box access to the API can be exploited to reconstruct private attributes and approximate input data, including vision tasks. Overall, these findings illustrate that privacy leakage through model outputs poses as high of a risk as training data membership and provides a clear motivation for DP and secure inference.

\subsection{Privacy-Preserving Mechanisms}

\subsubsection{Differential Privacy (DP)}
First, DP gives a statistical guarantee by adding a certain amount of noise to either the dataset itself or the features of models. According to the findings of Ziller et al., DP can be implemented using DP-SGD, enabling neural networks to be trained with information-theoretic privacy guarantees without compromising acceptable classification performance \cite{ziller}. In this regard, the use of Laplace or Gaussian noise makes it impossible to detect patient data as the basis for training, thereby preventing membership inference attacks on specific X-rays \cite{ziller}.

Implementing DP in a clinical setting faces many challenges due to the existing privacy-utility trade-off: applying DP with high noise to ensure privacy often leads to low accuracy. Recent innovations in the field have tried to improve DP by optimizing the approach in a way that abstractive representation of neural network computations reduces memory usage when dealing with per-sample gradients, traditionally computationally expensive in medical imaging \cite{ziller}.

Besides DP-SGD, various hybrid techniques have been explored to improve model robustness. One such method is PRICURE that combines DP with cryptography techniques to aggregate predictions with noisy masks, thus preventing semi-honest clients from making any inference attack \cite{jarin}. Similarly, the work of Abadi et al. introduced the Moments Accountant, which provides much better privacy loss bounds for models. This technique was later extended by the concept of RDP, which improves accuracy retention during training \cite{abadi}. It should be noted, however, that while the effect of DP is noticeable when applied directly to images at the pixel level, DP applied to latent feature embedding vectors is increasingly desirable in split-model architectures \cite{panzade}.

\subsubsection{Block-wise Image Encryption and Feature Permutation}
To address the performance issues caused by encryption, Block-wise Image Encryption (BIE) has been introduced recently. In this scheme, the image is encrypted by scrambling its tile pieces in a way that prevents identification of global anatomical features, while preserving local patterns needed for classification. According to the study by Kiya et al., Vision Transformers (ViT) work very efficiently with scrambled tiles since this neural network can detect pneumonia based on consolidation and the presence of fluid inside the tile pieces \cite{kiya}.

The scheme of BIE encrypts the images' structure, while SMPC makes sure that none of the entities ever sees the plain images. In the split-model approach, an encoder at the hospital transforms raw X-rays into feature vectors, which are further encrypted and split between several non-colluding servers. These servers are responsible for computation and return only the prediction to the client, without revealing any information about the model or its internal computations, as demonstrated in the PRICURE framework \cite{jarin}. This solution works perfectly well for semi-honest adversaries, who follow the protocol but try to extract sensitive data from the communication process.

\subsubsection{Secure Multi-Party Computation (SMPC)}
Secure Multi-Party Computation (SMPC) is a cryptographic methodology that allows several parties to jointly compute on their private datasets without disclosing the datasets. Unlike regular multi-party computing, which uses the plaintext representation of the input values, in SMPC, each input value is divided into several pieces called shares using a specific secret-sharing scheme. The shares are then distributed across non-colluding servers, who collectively execute the required calculations without obtaining the initial values.

A popular approach to implementing SMPC in machine learning uses two servers that store complementary shares of the data. To make the predictions, a client initially encodes an input image and extracts its features, which are then divided into secret shares and delivered to the servers. Servers execute the matrix multiplication and activation function computation on the shares of the images and return the final prediction to the client. Thus, during the inference, none of the intermediates or plaintext data is exposed to any third party.

The SecureML system demonstrates how SMPC can be used in both linear and neural networks using arithmetic secret sharing combined with non-linear protocols \cite{secureml}. In such systems, privacy guarantees depend on the semi-honest model, where all parties follow the protocol but seek to derive additional information from the results.

Nonetheless, the use of SMPC imposes a significant computational and communication overhead on the computation process due to the need for interactive protocols. For high-dimensional tasks like medical imaging, this may lead to a decrease in latency and poor scalability. Therefore, researchers now consider hybrid systems where SMPC is combined with other methodologies.

\section{System Architecture and Threat Model}
\label{sec:architecture}

\subsection{Split-Model Pipeline}
We adopt a two-party split-model architecture. The hospital hosts the encoder $E: \mathbb{R}^{150\times150\times 1}\!\to\!\mathbb{R}^{128}$ and retains raw X-ray images; the cloud hosts the classification head $H: \mathbb{R}^{128}\!\to\![0,1]$, which outputs the pneumonia probability. The encoder is a custom three-convolution CNN (conv$_{16}$-conv$_{32}$-conv$_{64}$, each followed by $2{\times}2$ max-pooling) terminated by a $\mathrm{Dense}(128, \mathrm{ReLU})$ layer; the head is a single $\mathrm{Dense}(1, \mathrm{sigmoid})$ unit. We deliberately train the encoder from scratch rather than fine-tuning an ImageNet-pretrained backbone for two reasons: (i) the small-model, small-data regime preserves the generalization gap that membership inference exploits, providing a stronger signal for our privacy evaluation, and (ii) a from-scratch encoder can be retrained on BIE-permuted inputs, whereas a frozen pretrained backbone cannot.

Each privacy-preserving transformation is applied at a specific layer of the pipeline:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{BIE} is applied to images \emph{before} the encoder; the encoder is retrained on permuted images.
  \item \textbf{DP} perturbs the 128-dimensional embedding \emph{after} the encoder and \emph{before} the head.
  \item \textbf{SMPC} splits the embedding into additive shares that are passed through the head; the shares are recombined inside the cloud.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Threat Models}
\label{sec:threats}

\paragraph{Membership Inference (black-box)} The adversary can submit X-ray queries to the deployed system and receive only the scalar confidence $H(E(x))$. Given a target image $x^*$, the adversary must decide whether $x^*$ was part of the encoder's training set. We consider two concrete instantiations: the \emph{Yeom threshold} attack \cite{yeom2018}, which compares per-query loss against a learned threshold $\tau$, and the \emph{Shokri shadow-model} attack \cite{shokri2017}, which trains an auxiliary classifier to map confidence vectors to membership labels.

\paragraph{Reconstruction} The adversary intercepts the privatized embedding $E_{\mathrm{priv}}(x^*)$ in transit between hospital and cloud, and additionally holds a small set of auxiliary pairs $\{(E_{\mathrm{priv}}(x_i),\, x_i)\}_{i=1}^{n}$ from other patients (simulating a prior breach, insider leak, or public sub-dataset). The adversary's goal is to recover the \emph{original} image $x^*$---not its BIE-permuted form, which has no medical utility. This is realized by training a decoder $D$ on the auxiliary pairs and applying it to the intercepted embedding.

Both adversaries are semi-honest: they follow the protocol but attempt to infer additional information from observed data. Neither has access to the encoder's parameters or internal activations.

\section{Methodology}
\label{sec:methodology}

\subsection{Privacy-Preserving Transformations}
\paragraph{Differential Privacy} We apply the Laplace mechanism \cite{dwork} independently to each coordinate of the 128-dimensional embedding:
\begin{equation}
  E_{\mathrm{DP}}(x) = E(x) + \mathrm{Lap}(0,\, \Delta/\epsilon)^{128},\label{eq:dp}
\end{equation}
where $\Delta$ is the $\ell_1$ sensitivity of $E$ (set to $1.0$ under standard clipping) and $\epsilon$ is the privacy budget. Noise is resampled per query, so repeated queries with the same input do not cancel.

\paragraph{Block-wise Image Encryption} Each $150{\times}150$ grayscale image is partitioned into a $15{\times}15$ grid of $10{\times}10$ tiles; tiles are permuted according to a deterministic permutation $\pi_k$ derived from key seed $k$. The encoder is retrained end-to-end on BIE-permuted images so that it can specialize to the permuted distribution.

\paragraph{Secure Multi-Party Computation} We implement 2-party additive secret sharing on the embedding: $E_{\mathrm{SMPC}}(x) = (s_1, s_2)$ with $s_1 + s_2 = E(x)$ and $s_1$ drawn uniformly. The head receives the reconstructed embedding; the cryptographic guarantee is that neither share alone reveals $E(x)$.

\subsection{Attack Implementations}

\paragraph{Yeom threshold} For each query $x$ with ground-truth label $y$, we compute the victim's binary cross-entropy loss $\ell(f(x), y)$ and select the threshold $\tau$ that maximizes balanced attack accuracy on the evaluation set. A query is classified as a \emph{member} if $\ell(f(x), y) < \tau$.

\paragraph{Shokri shadow-model} We train $N=5$ shadow models on disjoint bootstrap samples from a disjoint shadow pool, each with 500 in-distribution and 500 held-out queries. Shadow confidence vectors are labeled with ground-truth membership and pooled; an attack MLP (Dense$_{64}$-Dense$_{32}$-Dense$_{1}$) learns the decision boundary and is then applied to the victim's confidence vectors.

\paragraph{Reconstruction decoder} The decoder $D$ inverts a 128-dimensional embedding back to image space: $\mathrm{Dense}(7{\times}7{\times}128) \to \mathrm{Reshape}(7,7,128) \to$ four $\mathrm{Conv2DTranspose}$ layers with stride $2$ (upsampling $7{\to}14{\to}28{\to}56{\to}112$) $\to \mathrm{Resize}(150,150) \to \mathrm{Conv2D}(1, 3{\times}3, \mathrm{sigmoid})$. It is trained for 20 epochs with MSE loss and the Adam optimizer on $(E_{\mathrm{priv}}(x_i), x_i)$ auxiliary pairs, then evaluated against held-out original images using MSE, PSNR, and SSIM.

\section{Experimental Setup}
\label{sec:setup}

\subsection{Dataset and Splits}
We use the publicly available Kaggle \texttt{chest-xray-pneumonia} dataset \cite{mooney}, comprising 5\,856 grayscale chest radiographs labeled NORMAL or PNEUMONIA with an empirical class ratio of $2.70{:}1$ (PNEUMONIA:NORMAL). Rather than IID-pooling all images, we preserve Kaggle's native \texttt{train/}\!/\!\texttt{test/} subdirectory split, which is known to carry a mild distribution shift sufficient to induce a measurable train-test generalization gap \cite{mooney}. Concretely:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{victim\_members} (2\,000): stratified subsample of Kaggle \texttt{train/} (5\,216 images);
  \item \textbf{victim\_nonmembers} (624): all of Kaggle \texttt{test/};
  \item \textbf{shadow\_pool} (3\,216): remaining \texttt{train/} after the victim subsample.
\end{itemize}
Kaggle \texttt{val/} (16 images) is dropped. Splits are deterministic in the random seed. Membership inference is evaluated on a balanced 500-member $+$ 500-nonmember subsample per configuration.

\subsection{Hyperparameters}
All encoders and heads are trained for 10 epochs with Adam and batch size 32 on $150{\times}150$ grayscale inputs. PPT hyperparameters are fixed: DP with $\epsilon=1.0$, $\Delta=1.0$; BIE with tile size $10$ and key seed $7$; SMPC with $2$ additive shares. We run all $2^{3}=8$ PPT configurations under seed $42$. Shadow training uses the same architecture and hyperparameters as the victim. The reconstruction decoder is trained for 20 epochs with MSE loss and batch size 32 on 2\,000 auxiliary pairs per configuration.

\subsection{Metrics}
We report three metric families per configuration:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Utility}: test accuracy, $F_1$, and Expected Calibration Error (ECE);
  \item \textbf{Privacy}: Yeom and Shokri attack accuracy (balanced) and AUC; reconstruction MSE, PSNR (dB), and SSIM;
  \item \textbf{Efficiency}: per-configuration training latency, per-query inference latency, and peak memory.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Utility}
Baseline test accuracy is $0.788$. Adding DP alone costs $1.7$ points of accuracy ($0.771$), consistent with a moderate $\epsilon=1.0$ Laplace noise applied post-encoding. BIE incurs the largest isolated utility penalty ($-4.8$ points, $0.740$), reflecting the harder optimization problem of training an encoder on permuted inputs. SMPC alone, being lossless by construction, costs only $2.2$ points---attributable to training stochasticity rather than the mechanism itself. Combined configurations follow the worst-case of their constituents (e.g., dp+bie $= 0.752$, dominated by BIE's structural cost). All eight configurations remain within a $5$-point utility band, satisfying typical clinical-deployment tolerances for a defense study.

\subsection{Membership Inference Defense}
All configurations produce Yeom attack accuracies in $[0.614, 0.651]$ and Shokri accuracies in $[0.518, 0.553]$. Two unexpected patterns emerge. First, DP provides only a marginal reduction ($-0.005$ on Yeom, $-0.021$ on Shokri) at $\epsilon=1.0$. Second, and counter to the intuition that any defense should help, \emph{every BIE-containing configuration increases Yeom accuracy above the baseline}: bie-only reaches $0.647$ ($+0.028$), and bie+smpc peaks at $0.651$ ($+0.032$). SMPC-only is statistically indistinguishable from baseline, with a $+0.005$ delta well within seed noise.

These observations have a mechanistic explanation. MIA operates on the scalar output confidence $H(E_{\mathrm{priv}}(x))$. BIE is applied strictly upstream of the encoder, which is then retrained to compensate; the resulting model has \emph{higher} memorization on the permuted victim set (supported by a widened train-test gap visible in the ECE column: $0.227$ vs.~$0.183$ baseline), amplifying rather than attenuating the membership signal. SMPC reconstructs the original embedding inside the cloud before the head; the output confidence is bit-identical to the no-SMPC case modulo floating-point error. DP is the only mechanism that perturbs a quantity downstream of the encoder and upstream of the output, and is correspondingly the only mechanism that produces a consistent (if small) reduction.

\subsection{Reconstruction Defense}
In stark contrast to the MIA results, reconstruction PSNR partitions cleanly along a single variable---the presence of DP. All four DP-enabled configurations drop to $16.57$--$17.09$ dB ($-2.8$ to $-3.3$ dB versus baseline), while all four DP-free configurations remain within $\pm 0.2$ dB of baseline ($19.87$--$20.05$ dB). SSIM follows the same split ($0.42$--$0.43$ with DP, $0.50$--$0.51$ without), and MSE doubles under DP ($0.021$--$0.024$ vs.~$0.011$).

The failure of BIE in the reconstruction threat model is particularly illuminating. The decoder is trained on $(E_{\mathrm{priv}}(x_i), x_i)$ auxiliary pairs in which $x_i$ is the \emph{original} image, so whatever deterministic permutation BIE applies upstream is absorbed into the decoder's weights during training. BIE's security reduces to the secrecy of $\pi_k$; once the adversary holds even a modest number of auxiliary (embedding, original-image) pairs, $\pi_k$ is \emph{implicitly learned} as part of the inverse mapping. This is a Rosetta-Stone regime: known plaintexts collapse the permutation's hiding power without the attacker ever needing to recover $\pi_k$ explicitly. SMPC is equally inert for reconstruction: the adversary intercepts the recombined embedding at the head's input, which is identical to the non-SMPC case.

A subtler interaction is visible among the DP-enabled configurations themselves. DP alone achieves $16.57$ dB PSNR, whereas dp+bie rises to $16.98$ dB and all-three to $17.09$ dB---i.e., \emph{layering BIE on top of DP slightly erodes DP's reconstruction defense} by $\approx 0.4$--$0.5$ dB. Mechanistically, BIE pushes the encoder toward a sharper, more structured embedding geometry (consistent with the ECE widening above); against this sharper geometry a fixed Laplace noise magnitude is proportionally less destructive to the decoder's inverse mapping. SMPC combinations do not exhibit this effect, consistent with its non-interaction.

\subsection{Efficiency}
Training latency per configuration is in the range $136$--$200$ seconds; inference latency is $0.19$--$0.22$ ms per query; peak memory use during the run is $5.7$--$6.9$ GB. DP adds negligible overhead (noise is elementwise and sampled once per query). BIE adds $\approx 10\%$ to training latency due to per-batch permutation on the image tensor. SMPC's share-split on the 128-dimensional embedding is a single vector subtraction and is not measurable in wall-clock time at this scale.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\subsection{Threat-Model Mismatch Hypothesis}
The two kinds of attacks target entirely different surfaces of the pipeline. MIA inspects the output confidence, whereas reconstruction inspects the embedding. Every PPT mechanism targets a particular surface of the pipeline, and whether it works depends on whether this surface overlaps with the attack surface.

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{DP} applies perturbation directly to the embedding. As a result, it influences the head (thus making MIA impossible) and is delivered directly to the adversary without any modification (thus making reconstruction impossible). DP is the only mechanism that works against both attack surfaces.
  \item \textbf{BIE} applies perturbation directly to the image. The encoder tries to correct the perturbation by training itself again, thus recovering or, as we show, improving the confidence in the output prediction. The security assumptions behind BIE include key secrecy, which fails against the reconstruction adversary.
  \item \textbf{SMPC} applies perturbation in transit from one share-holder to another one. The reconstructed embedding is still perfect, therefore neither MIA nor reconstruction can observe perturbation in action.
\end{itemize}

This discrepancy is captured in Table~\ref{tab:mismatch}. Our point is not that either BIE or SMPC fails to protect data in general; both methods are robust enough to the threat models against which they were designed. In particular, BIE is guaranteed to be resilient to naive eavesdropping attacks at the image level, while SMPC is provably secure under the assumption of observing one share. However, it is the case that neither of these assumptions holds for the adversarial models we consider, and applying either method in such settings would be an illusion of security.

\subsection{Practical Implications}
As far as practical application of our pipeline split model to a practitioner is concerned, the most efficient method of combating adversarial attacks by means of any technique during inference time can be achieved by employing embedding level differential privacy. In this setting, DP directly targets the part of the pipeline that both MIA and reconstruction attacks rely on, making it uniquely effective compared to other single-mechanism defenses.

BIE and SMPC on the other hand, should be interpreted as complementary tools designed for different threat scenarios rather than as substitutes for output-level privacy. For example, BIE is better suited to defending against straightforward image-level interception, while SMPC is intended to protect against partial information leakage across distributed parties. When applied outside those assumptions, their protective value against inference-time adversaries becomes limited.

Moreover, combining all three mechanisms in a straightforward ``stacked'' configuration does not yield additional protection against either reconstruction or membership inference beyond what DP alone already provides. Instead, it introduces compounded costs in model utility, as reflected in the drop in test accuracy (both dp+bie and the all-three configuration fall to 0.752).

\subsection{Limitations}
There are three main limitations in our research that weaken our results. First, the value of $\epsilon = 1.0$ is fixed; the sensitivity analysis could help us better explore the entire tradeoff between privacy and utility and could potentially demonstrate better MIA resistance at lower budgets. Second, the size of our data is relatively small ($5\,856$ images), and the data comes from one source only; the performance of MIA attacks on other data sources might vary, especially for BIE. Third, we use $19.88$ dB PSNR as the starting point for our reconstruction attacks; high-PSNR reconstruction (e.g., above $30$ dB) would highlight the importance of the $3$-dB PSNR drop due to DP more.

\section{Conclusion}
These transformations (Differential Privacy, Block-wise Image Encryption, and Secure Multi-Party Computation) are evaluated empirically against membership inference and reconstruction attacks on a split-model pneumonia chest X-ray classifier using all eight configurations. The core result is a defense efficacy principle: there is a misspecification mismatch between defense mechanisms and the threat model; embedding-level differential privacy protects against both sides of the attack surface and achieves substantial reconstruction defense ($-3.2$ dB PSNR) and poor MIA defense ($\epsilon=1.0$ with a mere $1.7$ utility reduction); image-level block-wise encryption and transit-level multi-party computation are imperceptible from an inference-time attacker who looks at confidences post-encoder or recombined embeddings, and the former could even make membership attacks worse by facilitating memorization.

More broadly, these results highlight that the location within the pipeline where a defense is applied is just as important as the defense itself. Mechanisms that operate before or during intermediate stages may offer strong guarantees under their intended assumptions, yet still fail to meaningfully interfere with what an inference-time adversary actually observes. This disconnect explains why BIE and SMPC, despite their theoretical strengths, do not translate into practical protection in our evaluated setting.

Those building pipeline defenses using split-model classifiers must pay attention to output-level privacy and recognize that stacking image-level and transit-level defenses is ineffective for defending against inference-time attackers. In practice, prioritizing defenses that directly perturb or constrain the information exposed at the model's outputs is likely to yield more reliable protection than combining multiple mechanisms that act on disconnected parts of the pipeline.
