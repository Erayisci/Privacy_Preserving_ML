\begin{abstract}
Deploying deep learning models for chest X-ray pneumonia classification on third-party cloud infrastructure exposes sensitive patient imagery to membership inference and model-inversion attacks. We propose a split-model architecture where a hospital-side encoder with three convolutional layers produces 128-dimensional embeddings for a lightweight classification head hosted in the cloud. Our study systematically evaluates three privacy-preserving techniques: Differential Privacy (DP) applying Laplace noise to embeddings, Block-wise Image Encryption (BIE) on input images, and Secure Multi-Party Computation (SMPC) via additive secret sharing. We test all eight possible combinations of these methods using the Kaggle chest X-ray pneumonia dataset. Each configuration is subjected to Yeom and Shokri membership inference attacks alongside a decoder-based reconstruction attack. Each defense is evaluated under its own design threat model: an embedding-leak adversary for DP and BIE, and a single corrupted non-colluding server for SMPC. Under this threat-model-consistent evaluation, two mechanisms succeed at reconstruction. DP reduces reconstruction PSNR by 3.2 dB \emph{without} utility cost; in fact, it yields a $+2.2$ point accuracy gain consistent with noise-induced regularization at $\epsilon=1.0$ and additionally provides a small reduction in Yeom MIA. SMPC drives single-server reconstruction PSNR to $\approx 15.5$ dB, below even the no-information mean-image baseline, with the attacker's view statistically independent of the true embedding (Pearson $r \approx 0.003$). BIE fails at reconstruction because an adversary with auxiliary (embedding, original-image) pairs implicitly learns the permutation; it also slightly amplifies Yeom MIA via increased memorization on permuted inputs. We conclude that defense efficacy is determined by the alignment between the layer at which a mechanism intervenes and the threat model it is evaluated against; stacking mechanisms across mismatched threat models does not compound protection but combining DP and SMPC is justified when both an embedding-leak and a single-server-compromise adversary must be defended simultaneously.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
The rapid adoption of artificial intelligence in healthcare has forced a fundamental rethink of data governance strategies. This transition replaces traditional, human-led oversight with automated systems that must navigate the inherent tension between data utility and patient confidentiality \cite{b1}. Older frameworks often relied on rigid, manual protocols that created significant operational bottlenecks. In contrast, modern clinical environments are shifting toward intelligent platforms that classify sensitive information dynamically and adjust security measures based on the immediate context of the data. Such an evolution is vital for maintaining strict regulatory compliance without sacrificing the speed or quality of essential medical services \cite{b1}.

However, the current reliance on cloud-based deep learning for medical imaging, particularly in chest X-ray analysis, introduces a significant vulnerability: the exposure of raw, personally identifiable patient data to third-party servers. In a typical centralized pipeline, raw and personally identifiable patient imagery is transmitted to third-party servers for processing. While these models offer high diagnostic accuracy, the legal and ethical risks associated with potential data breaches have created an urgent need for Privacy-Preserving Machine Learning (PPML) solutions. The goal of these technologies is to decouple diagnostic utility from data visibility, ensuring that the cloud can process information without actually "seeing" the sensitive patient details.


In this paper (i), we address these challenges by implementing a split-model chest X-ray pneumonia classifier designed to ensure that raw images never leave the hospital infrastructure. We systematically evaluate three privacy-preserving transformations, including Differential Privacy, Block-wise Image Encryption, and Secure Multi-Party Computation, by testing them in all eight possible combinations. We then measure the residual privacy leakage of each specific configuration through two membership inference attacks and a decoder-based reconstruction attack. Our results highlight that each defense mechanism targets a distinct adversarial surface. We show that combining these methods naively does not yield cumulative protection; rather, the specific architectural layer at which a defense operates determines its ability to influence different attack families.


\section{Literature Review}
The rapid progress in deep learning has established medical imaging as a cornerstone of modern diagnostics. Applications now range from automated tumor detection to the precise segmentation of pathological structures. However, these advancements in imaging and data transmission have introduced significant privacy risks. This necessitates the development of robust encryption and protection algorithms capable of handling the high redundancy inherent in medical image data \cite{panzade}. The traditional centralized approach to AI, where vast amounts of patient data are funneled into a single cloud server, creates legal and ethical bottlenecks. Hospitals currently face a conflict between the need for high-performance cloud diagnostics and the strict mandates of HIPAA and GDPR, which prohibit sharing unencrypted, identifiable data. This tension has driven the rise of Privacy-Preserving Machine Learning (PPML), a field focused on developing models that can detect disease without exposing the patient's identity.

The foundational mechanisms of PPML are often categorized into four primary pillars tailored for the medical domain \cite{panzade}. Federated Learning (FL) serves as a decentralized training paradigm where institutions collaboratively train global models by exchanging only local updates, such as weights or gradients, rather than raw images. This ensures sensitive data remains behind local firewalls, reducing the risks associated with central storage. Differential Privacy (DP) complements this by providing a statistical guarantee of privacy through the injection of controlled noise into datasets or model parameters. This mechanism ensures that the presence of any single patient record does not significantly alter the model's output, effectively mitigating re-identification or membership inference attacks \cite{panzade}.

In addition to decentralization and statistical noise, cryptographic techniques provide even greater privacy by performing computations with raw data hidden from the host system. These include homomorphic encryption (HE), which allows performing math on encrypted data in a way that even the model cannot obtain any information from the image. Likewise, secure multi-party computation (SMPC) allows performing computations in the same way, except that the data is split into different parts (``shares'') that can be used to reconstruct the original object once decrypted. SMPC has been extensively discussed as an encryption-based PPML solution, with a variety of frameworks available for training machine learning algorithms while distributing sensitive data across non-colluding servers \cite{secureml}. For instance, SecureML can train both linear and neural network models using data represented as secret shares and perform computations using secure arithmetic operations. Although SMPC provides much better privacy than previous MPC-based models and significantly improves scalability, it introduces new computational and communication costs, thereby limiting its practicality for medical imaging applications.

Another innovation in the field is introduced by the Swarm Learning technique which allows fully decentralized training without a central orchestrator by integrating PPML with blockchain and peer-to-peer networking techniques \cite{panzade}.

Finally, while there is a range of PPML techniques for ML data, Panzade et al. (2024) highlight the critical ``privacy-utility trade-off'' involved in implementing PPML technologies \cite{panzade}. In addition to membership inference attacks described above, model inversion attacks represent another major threat. Specifically, as demonstrated by Fredrikson et al., an adversary can perform model inversion by utilizing access to the model's output to extract features from sensitive input data \cite{fredrikson}. Their research reveals that even the black-box access to the API can be exploited to reconstruct private attributes and approximate input data, including vision tasks. Overall, these findings illustrate that privacy leakage through model outputs poses as high of a risk as training data membership and provides a clear motivation for DP and secure inference.

\subsection{Privacy-Preserving Mechanisms}
\subsubsection{Differential Privacy (DP)}
First, DP gives a statistical guarantee by adding a certain amount of noise to either the dataset itself or the features of models. According to the findings of Ziller et al., DP can be implemented using DP-SGD, enabling neural networks to be trained with information-theoretic privacy guarantees without compromising acceptable classification performance \cite{ziller}. In this regard, the use of Laplace or Gaussian noise makes it impossible to detect patient data as the basis for training, thereby preventing membership inference attacks on specific X-rays \cite{ziller}.

Implementing DP in a clinical setting faces many challenges due to the existing privacy-utility trade-off: applying DP with high noise to ensure privacy often leads to low accuracy. Recent innovations in the field have tried to improve DP by optimizing the approach in a way that abstractive representation of neural network computations reduces memory usage when dealing with per-sample gradients, traditionally computationally expensive in medical imaging \cite{ziller}.

Other research has explored hybrid approaches to improve robustness. For instance, the PRICURE framework integrates DP with cryptographic techniques to mask prediction results via noisy aggregation, specifically deterring semi-honest clients from mounting inference attacks \cite{jarin}. Abadi et al. introduced the Moments Accountant technique to enable tighter accounting of cumulative privacy loss, a concept later refined through Rényi Differential Privacy (RDP). A parallel line of work in split-model deployments applies DP at the latent-feature level rather than at raw pixels, trading a smaller absolute perturbation for preserved global anatomical structure. This is the design point we adopt in our methodology.
Besides DP-SGD, various hybrid techniques have been explored to improve model robustness. One such method is PRICURE that combines DP with cryptography techniques to aggregate predictions with noisy masks, thus preventing semi-honest clients from making any inference attack \cite{jarin}. Similarly, the work of Abadi et al. introduced the Moments Accountant, which provides much better privacy loss bounds for models. This technique was later extended by the concept of RDP, which improves accuracy retention during training \cite{abadi}. It should be noted, however, that while the effect of DP is noticeable when applied directly to images at the pixel level, DP applied to latent feature embedding vectors is increasingly desirable in split-model architectures \cite{panzade}.

\subsubsection{Block-wise Image Encryption (BIE)}
To reduce the performance degradation often seen with traditional encryption, recent studies have proposed Block-wise Image Encryption. This technique shuffles image tiles using a secret key to obfuscate global anatomical landmarks while preserving the local texture patterns required for classification. Kiya et al. show that Vision Transformers are particularly adept at processing these permuted images without significant loss in accuracy, as the model identifies pneumonia through consolidation patterns within the intact tiles \cite{kiya}.

The scheme of BIE encrypts the images' structure, while SMPC makes sure that none of the entities ever sees the plain images. In the split-model approach, an encoder at the hospital transforms raw X-rays into feature vectors, which are further encrypted and split between several non-colluding servers. These servers are responsible for computation and return only the prediction to the client, without revealing any information about the model or its internal computations, as demonstrated in the PRICURE framework \cite{jarin}. This solution works perfectly well for semi-honest adversaries, who follow the protocol but try to extract sensitive data from the communication process.

\subsubsection{Secure Multi-Party Computation (SMPC)}
Secure Multi-Party Computation (SMPC) is a cryptographic methodology that allows several parties to jointly compute on their private datasets without disclosing the datasets. Unlike regular multi-party computing, which uses the plaintext representation of the input values, in SMPC, each input value is divided into several pieces called shares using a specific secret-sharing scheme. The shares are then distributed across non-colluding servers, who collectively execute the required calculations without obtaining the initial values.

A popular approach to implementing SMPC in machine learning uses two servers that store complementary shares of the data. To make the predictions, a client initially encodes an input image and extracts its features, which are then divided into secret shares and delivered to the servers. Servers execute the matrix multiplication and activation function computation on the shares of the images and return the final prediction to the client. Thus, during the inference, none of the intermediates or plaintext data is exposed to any third party.

The SecureML system demonstrates how SMPC can be used in both linear and neural networks using arithmetic secret sharing combined with non-linear protocols \cite{secureml}. In such systems, privacy guarantees depend on the semi-honest model, where all parties follow the protocol but seek to derive additional information from the results.

Nonetheless, the use of SMPC imposes a significant computational and communication overhead on the computation process due to the need for interactive protocols. For high-dimensional tasks like medical imaging, this may lead to a decrease in latency and poor scalability. Therefore, researchers now consider hybrid systems where SMPC is combined with other methodologies.

\section{System Architecture and Threat Model}
\label{sec:architecture}

\subsection{Split-Model Pipeline}
We adopt a two-party split-model architecture. The hospital hosts the encoder $E: \mathbb{R}^{150\times150\times 1}\!\to\!\mathbb{R}^{128}$ and retains raw X-ray images; the cloud hosts the classification head $H: \mathbb{R}^{128}\!\to\![0,1]$, which outputs the pneumonia probability. The encoder is a custom three-convolution CNN (conv$_{16}$-conv$_{32}$-conv$_{64}$, each followed by $2{\times}2$ max-pooling) terminated by a $\mathrm{Dense}(128, \mathrm{ReLU})$ layer; the head is a single $\mathrm{Dense}(1, \mathrm{sigmoid})$ unit. We deliberately train the encoder from scratch rather than fine-tuning an ImageNet-pretrained backbone for two reasons: (i) the small-model, small-data regime preserves the generalization gap that membership inference exploits, providing a stronger signal for our privacy evaluation, and (ii) a from-scratch encoder can be retrained on BIE-permuted inputs, whereas a frozen pretrained backbone cannot.

This split is not motivated by computational offloadingin our architecture the encoder dominates inference costbut by the need to construct an explicit \emph{trust boundary} that makes inference-time privacy mechanisms meaningful to evaluate. If both encoder and head were colocated at the hospital, no patient-derived value would ever cross a network or be observed by an external party, and there would be no surface on which to apply DP, BIE, or SMPC. If the encoder were placed in the cloud, raw imagery would have to be transmitted, eliminating the privacy problem we set out to study. The two-party split is therefore the minimal configuration that creates a well-defined cloud-side observation point while preserving the regulatory constraint (HIPAA/GDPR data locality) that raw images remain at the hospital. We do not claim this split is the only realistic deployment topology; rather, it is the experimental scaffold that allows us to ask, in a controlled setting, ``given that some inference work must be performed by an external party, what does each privacy-preserving transformation actually buy?'' This framing should be kept in mind when interpreting our results: they characterize the inference-time threat surface of split-model deployments rather than prescribing a specific deployment architecture. In our pipeline, the hospital performs the full end-to-end training (encoder and head jointly) on its local data and then transfers only the trained head weights to the cloud for inference; the cloud never observes training data, embeddings, or labels during the training phase. This avoids a separate training-time leakage channel and lets us isolate inference-time privacy as the object of study.

Each privacy-preserving transformation is applied at a specific layer of the pipeline:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{BIE} is applied to images \emph{before} the encoder; the encoder is retrained on permuted images.
  \item \textbf{DP} perturbs the 128-dimensional embedding \emph{after} the encoder and \emph{before} the head.
  \item \textbf{SMPC} splits the embedding into additive shares that are passed through the head; the shares are recombined inside the cloud.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Threat Models}
\label{sec:threats}

\paragraph{Membership Inference (black-box)} The adversary can submit X-ray queries to the deployed system and receive only the scalar confidence $H(E(x))$. Given a target image $x^*$, the adversary must decide whether $x^*$ was part of the encoder's training set. We consider two concrete instantiations: the \emph{Yeom threshold} attack \cite{yeom2018}, which compares per-query loss against a learned threshold $\tau$, and the \emph{Shokri shadow-model} attack \cite{shokri2017}, which trains an auxiliary classifier to map confidence vectors to membership labels.

\paragraph{Reconstruction} The adversary intercepts the privatized embedding $E_{\mathrm{priv}}(x^*)$ in transit between hospital and cloud, and additionally holds a small set of auxiliary pairs $\{(E_{\mathrm{priv}}(x_i),\, x_i)\}_{i=1}^{n}$ from other patients (simulating a prior breach, insider leak, or public sub-dataset). The adversary's goal is to recover the \emph{original} image $x^*$not its BIE-permuted form, which has no medical utility. This is realized by training a decoder $D$ on the auxiliary pairs and applying it to the intercepted embedding.

Both adversaries are semi-honest: they follow the protocol but attempt to infer additional information from observed data. Neither has access to the encoder's parameters or internal activations.

\section{Methodology}
\label{sec:methodology}

\subsection{Privacy-Preserving Transformations}
\paragraph{Differential Privacy} We apply the Laplace mechanism \cite{dwork} independently to each coordinate of the 128-dimensional embedding:
\begin{equation}
  E_{\mathrm{DP}}(x) = E(x) + \mathrm{Lap}(0,\, \Delta/\epsilon)^{128},\label{eq:dp}
\end{equation}
where $\Delta$ is the $\ell_1$ sensitivity of $E$, taken as $1.0$ as a working assumption (embeddings are not explicitly clipped; see Section~\ref{sec:limitations}), and $\epsilon$ is the privacy budget. Noise is resampled per query, so repeated queries with the same input do not cancel.

\paragraph{Block-wise Image Encryption} Each $150{\times}150$ grayscale image is partitioned into a $15{\times}15$ grid of $10{\times}10$ tiles; tiles are permuted according to a deterministic permutation $\pi_k$ derived from key seed $k$. The encoder is retrained end-to-end on BIE-permuted images so that it can specialize to the permuted distribution.

\paragraph{Secure Multi-Party Computation} We implement 2-party additive secret sharing on the embedding: $E_{\mathrm{SMPC}}(x) = (s_1, s_2)$ with $s_1 + s_2 = E(x)$ and $s_1$ drawn from a distribution independent of $E(x)$ (a standard Gaussian in our implementation). Each server in the protocol sees only its own share; the head reconstructs the embedding from the two shares before classification. Strictly, additive secret sharing has information-theoretic security only over a finite ring with uniform masks; our Gaussian floating-point implementation is a continuous-domain simulation of this. Empirically, a single share has Pearson $r \approx 0.003$ against $E(x)$ at the mask scale we use (Section~\ref{sec:limitations}). We additionally implement, but do not run in the matrix, a full Beaver-triple-based 2-party SMPC protocol (\texttt{SMPCInferenceProtocol} in the codebase) in which the head weights $W$, $b$ are also additively split across servers and the matrix multiplication is performed via Beaver triples generated by the client; we omit this from the matrix only because its inference output is mathematically equal to the standard reconstruct-then-classify pipeline above. To evaluate SMPC under its actual semi-honest single-corrupted-server threat model, we additionally measure reconstruction quality from one server's share alone (Section~\ref{sec:results}).

\subsection{Attack Implementations}

\paragraph{Yeom threshold} For each query $x$ with ground-truth label $y$, we compute the victim's binary cross-entropy loss $\ell(f(x), y)$ and select the threshold $\tau$ that maximizes attack accuracy on the evaluation set, which is balanced 50/50 by construction (Section~\ref{sec:setup}). A query is classified as a \emph{member} if $\ell(f(x), y) < \tau$.

\paragraph{Shokri shadow-model} We train $N=5$ shadow models, each on an independently sampled stratified (train, holdout) split of the shadow pool: $500$ in-distribution and $500$ held-out queries per shadow. The shadow pool is itself disjoint from both the victim's training set and the MIA evaluation set. Each victim probability $p$ is expanded into the 2-d feature $[p, 1-p]$; shadow features are labeled with ground-truth membership and pooled across shadows. An attack MLP ($\mathrm{Input}_{2}$-Dense$_{64}$-Dense$_{32}$-Dense$_{1}$) learns the decision boundary and is then applied to the victim's confidence vectors.

\paragraph{Reconstruction decoder} The decoder $D$ inverts a 128-dimensional embedding back to image space: $\mathrm{Dense}(7{\times}7{\times}128) \to \mathrm{Reshape}(7,7,128) \to$ four $\mathrm{Conv2DTranspose}$ layers with stride $2$ and filters $(128, 64, 32, 16)$ (upsampling $7{\to}14{\to}28{\to}56{\to}112$) $\to \mathrm{Resize}(150,150) \to \mathrm{Conv2D}(1, 3{\times}3, \mathrm{sigmoid})$. It is trained for 20 epochs with MSE loss and the Adam optimizer on $(E_{\mathrm{priv}}(x_i), x_i)$ auxiliary pairs, then evaluated against held-out original images using MSE, PSNR, and SSIM.

\section{Experimental Setup}
\label{sec:setup}

\subsection{Dataset and Splits}
We use the publicly available Kaggle \texttt{chest-xray-pneumonia} dataset \cite{mooney}, comprising 5\,856 grayscale chest radiographs labeled NORMAL or PNEUMONIA with an empirical class ratio of $2.70{:}1$ (PNEUMONIA:NORMAL). Rather than IID-pooling all images, we preserve Kaggle's native \texttt{train/}\!/\!\texttt{test/} subdirectory split, which is known to carry a mild distribution shift sufficient to induce a measurable train-test generalization gap \cite{mooney}. Concretely:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{victim\_members} (2\,000): stratified subsample of Kaggle \texttt{train/} (5\,216 images);
  \item \textbf{victim\_nonmembers} (624): all of Kaggle \texttt{test/};
  \item \textbf{shadow\_pool} (3\,216): remaining \texttt{train/} after the victim subsample.
\end{itemize}
Kaggle \texttt{val/} (16 images) is dropped. Splits are deterministic in the random seed. Membership inference is evaluated on a balanced 500-member $+$ 500-nonmember subsample per configuration.

\subsection{Hyperparameters}
All encoders and heads are trained for 10 epochs with Adam and batch size 32 on $150{\times}150$ grayscale inputs. PPT hyperparameters are fixed: DP with $\epsilon=1.0$, $\Delta=1.0$; BIE with tile size $10$ and key seed $7$; SMPC with $2$ additive shares. We run all $2^{3}=8$ PPT configurations under seed $42$. Shadow training uses the same architecture and hyperparameters as the victim. The reconstruction decoder is trained for 20 epochs with MSE loss and batch size 32 on 2\,000 auxiliary pairs per configuration.

\subsection{Metrics}
We report three metric families per configuration:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Utility}: test accuracy, $F_1$, and Expected Calibration Error (ECE);
  \item \textbf{Privacy}: Yeom and Shokri attack accuracy (balanced) and AUC; reconstruction MSE, PSNR (dB), and SSIM;
  \item \textbf{Efficiency}: per-configuration training latency, per-query inference latency, and peak memory.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Utility}
Baseline test accuracy is $0.723$. Two patterns dominate the utility column. \emph{First, DP does not degrade utility; it improves it.} Every DP-enabled configuration sits at $0.745$--$0.747$, a $+2.2$ to $+2.4$ point gain over baseline, with a corresponding ECE reduction (from $0.241$ baseline to $0.212$ for dp-only). At $\epsilon=1.0$ with $\Delta=1.0$, the per-coordinate Laplace scale is small relative to the magnitude of ReLU-activated embeddings, but large enough to act as a stochastic regularizer during head retraining, a known phenomenon analogous to dropout or noise injection \cite{ziller}. \emph{Second, SMPC is bit-identical to baseline.} The pairs (baseline, smpc-only), (dp-only, dp+smpc), (bie-only, bie+smpc), and (dp+bie, all-three) collapse onto identical utility numbers (matching to three decimal places on Test Acc / $F_1$ / ECE), confirming the lossless-construction claim: share-then-reconstruct is mathematically a no-op modulo float32 rounding. BIE alone moves accuracy by only $+0.6$ points ($0.729$), well within seed noise; the large BIE utility penalty observed in earlier pilot runs disappears once the encoder is retrained on permuted inputs to convergence. The full eight-configuration utility band is now $0.723$--$0.747$ (2.4 points), comfortably within clinical-deployment tolerances.

\subsection{Membership Inference Defense}
All configurations produce Yeom attack accuracies in $[0.625, 0.652]$ and Shokri accuracies in $[0.516, 0.539]$. Three patterns emerge. \emph{First, DP yields a small but consistent Yeom reduction} ($-0.015$ versus baseline; $0.640 \to 0.625$ for dp-only) and is the only mechanism that meaningfully shifts Yeom in the privacy-preserving direction. For DP-without-BIE configurations the Shokri delta is essentially zero ($\pm 0.002$); dp+bie and all-three sit lower at $0.516$, but the full Shokri range across all eight configurations is only $0.023$ wide and not monotone in any single mechanism, so we read the shadow-model attack as dominated by features other than the perturbation we inject at $\epsilon=1.0$. \emph{Second, every BIE-without-DP configuration sits at the top of the Yeom range}: bie-only and bie+smpc both reach $0.652$ ($+0.012$ above baseline); when DP is layered on top, dp+bie and all-three land at $0.635$, eroding the DP-only Yeom gain. \emph{Third, SMPC is exactly compositional}: smpc-only matches baseline ($0.640$), dp+smpc matches dp-only ($0.625$), bie+smpc matches bie-only ($0.652$), and all-three matches dp+bie ($0.635$).

The mechanistic explanation is unchanged from earlier analyses but the new numbers tighten the story. MIA operates on the scalar output confidence $H(E_{\mathrm{priv}}(x))$. BIE is applied strictly upstream of the encoder, which is then retrained to compensate; the resulting model has \emph{higher} memorization on the permuted victim set (visible in the ECE column: bie-only and bie+smpc reach $0.249$ vs.~$0.241$ baseline; the dp+bie and all-three configurations fall back to $0.231$, indicating that DP's regularization absorbs the BIE-induced sharpening), amplifying rather than attenuating the membership signal. SMPC reconstructs the original embedding inside the cloud before the head; the output confidence is bit-identical to the no-SMPC case modulo floating-point error, which is why the four SMPC pairs collapse exactly. DP is the only mechanism that perturbs a quantity downstream of the encoder and upstream of the output, and is correspondingly the only mechanism that produces a consistent reduction in Yeom.

\subsection{Reconstruction Defense}
Each defense in the matrix is evaluated against the reconstruction adversary it was \emph{designed} to defeat: DP and BIE against an adversary who observes the (privatized) embedding delivered to the head, and SMPC against an adversary who controls a single non-colluding server and observes only that server's additive share. Under this threat-model-consistent evaluation, reconstruction PSNR partitions cleanly: defense is strong whenever \emph{either} DP or SMPC is enabled, and weak whenever neither is. The two SMPC-without-DP configurations (smpc-only, bie+smpc) reach $15.5$ dB; the two DP-without-SMPC configurations (dp-only, dp+bie) reach $16.4$--$17.2$ dB; the two configurations with both (dp+smpc, all-three) sit at the SMPC level ($15.4$--$15.5$ dB), as the single-server view is the binding constraint. Only the two configurations with neither mechanism (baseline, bie-only) leak heavily at $19.6$--$20.1$ dB.

The failure of BIE in the reconstruction threat model is illuminating. The decoder is trained on $(E_{\mathrm{priv}}(x_i), x_i)$ auxiliary pairs in which $x_i$ is the \emph{original} image, so whatever deterministic permutation BIE applies upstream is absorbed into the decoder's weights during training. BIE's security reduces to the secrecy of $\pi_k$; once the adversary holds even a modest number of auxiliary (embedding, original-image) pairs, $\pi_k$ is \emph{implicitly learned} as part of the inverse mapping. This is a Rosetta-Stone regime: known plaintexts collapse the permutation's hiding power without the attacker ever needing to recover $\pi_k$ explicitly.

\subsubsection{Why SMPC Wins Reconstruction in Its Own Threat Model}
SMPC's reconstruction defense rests on an information-theoretic property, not a noise budget. In our 2-party additive sharing, the share given to the corrupted server is sampled from a distribution that does not depend on the true embedding $E(x)$. Table~\ref{tab:single_server} verifies this empirically: the Pearson correlation $r$ between the attacker's share and the true embedding $E(x)$, a direct empirical proxy for SMPC's information-theoretic independence guarantee, is $\approx 0.003$--$0.004$ across all four SMPC configurations, indistinguishable from zero given the sample size and consistent with the theoretical guarantee of independence under the non-collusion assumption. The single-server (SS) PSNR reproduced from Table~\ref{tab:results} is below the no-information mean-image baseline ($16.92$ dB) for every SMPC configuration, confirming that the single-server attack collapses to less than no-information performance. With the attacker's input thus carrying no signal about $E(x)$, a decoder trained on those shares can only memorize the marginal pixel distribution of its training images. Reconstruction PSNR collapses to $\approx 15.5$ dB, \emph{below} the trivial mean-image baseline ($16.92$ dB), because the decoder has fit to spurious correlations in random noise rather than to a useful inverse mapping.

A subtler interaction is visible among the DP-only and DP+BIE configurations. DP alone achieves $16.44$ dB PSNR, whereas dp+bie rises to $17.15$ dB, i.e., \emph{layering BIE on top of DP erodes DP's reconstruction defense} by $\approx 0.7$ dB. Mechanistically, BIE pushes the encoder toward a sharper, more structured embedding geometry (consistent with the ECE widening above); against this sharper geometry a fixed Laplace noise magnitude is proportionally less destructive to the decoder's inverse mapping. The dp+smpc and all-three configurations do not exhibit this effect because the SMPC threat model dominates, replacing the embedding the attacker sees with a random share long before any embedding-geometry effects can matter.

\subsection{Efficiency}
Encoder training latency, when not served from cache, is $\approx 22$--$27$ seconds (encoder hash depends on the BIE flag, key, and seed; DP and SMPC do not invalidate the encoder cache, so within a fixed seed only two encoders are trained across the eight configurations, one for BIE on and one for BIE off). Per-configuration head training plus attack execution is dominated by shadow-model training; head retraining on noisy embeddings (DP-enabled configs) adds a fraction of a second. Inference latency is $0.19$--$0.22$ ms per query and is indistinguishable across configurations. SMPC's share-split on the 128-dimensional embedding is a single vector subtraction and is not measurable in wall-clock time at this scale; the full Beaver-triple variant adds $\approx 200$--$500\,\mu$s per inference call but is not used in the matrix runs since its output is bit-identical to the simulation.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\subsection{Threat-Model Mismatch Hypothesis}
The two kinds of attacks target entirely different surfaces of the pipeline. MIA inspects the output confidence, whereas reconstruction inspects the embedding. Every PPT mechanism targets a particular surface of the pipeline, and whether it works depends on whether this surface overlaps with the attack surface.

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{DP} applies perturbation directly to the embedding. As a result, it influences both the head's output (slightly weakening MIA) and the embedding the reconstruction adversary intercepts (substantially weakening reconstruction). DP is effective against both attack surfaces in the same threat modelan embedding-leak adversary.
  \item \textbf{BIE} applies perturbation directly to the image. The encoder retrains end-to-end to compensate, so the resulting embedding is not meaningfully different from the non-BIE case in either MIA confidence or reconstruction quality; the only second-order effect is a small increase in memorization on permuted inputs, which slightly amplifies Yeom MIA.
  \item \textbf{SMPC} provides information-theoretic independence between any single server's view and the true embedding. Against its design threat modela single corrupted non-colluding serverSMPC is the strongest reconstruction defense in our matrix (PSNR $\approx 15.5$ dB, below the no-information baseline). Against MIA, SMPC is a no-op: the head reconstructs the embedding before classification, so the output confidence carries no SMPC perturbation.
\end{itemize}

This taxonomy is summarized in Table~\ref{tab:mismatch}, which pairs each mechanism's intervention layer with its defense outcome on each attack surface under its own threat model (embedding-leak adversary for DP and BIE; single corrupted non-colluding server for SMPC); a mechanism defends an attack only when its threat-model assumptions hold and its intervention layer is downstream ofor coincident withthe attacker's observation point. Our point is not that BIE fails to protect data in general; BIE is provably resilient to naive eavesdropping attacks that observe only the encrypted image and have no auxiliary (embedding, original-image) pairs. However, our reconstruction adversary holds exactly such pairs, so BIE's key-secrecy assumption does not buy useful protection here. SMPC, by contrast, is correctly evaluated against the adversary it was designed fora single corrupted non-colluding serverand the table reflects that match.

\subsection{Practical Implications}
The practitioner's choice of defense should be driven by the threat model the deployment actually faces. For a deployment in which the cloud is fully trusted to compute the head but a passive adversary may intercept embeddings in transit or via a leak, embedding-level DP is uniquely effective because it perturbs the same quantity the adversary observes; it is also the only mechanism in our matrix that meaningfully (if modestly) shifts MIA in the privacy-preserving direction. For a deployment that distributes inference across two non-colluding servers, SMPC delivers stronger reconstruction defense than DP under the corresponding single-corrupted-server adversary, while leaving output confidences unchanged and therefore offering no MIA protection. The two defenses thus address disjoint operational risks: DP for embedding-leak adversaries, SMPC for single-server-compromise adversaries. Combining them is sensible if the deployment must guard against both.

BIE, by contrast, is designed for a different threat model entirelyan adversary who observes only encrypted images and has no auxiliary (embedding, original-image) pairs. In our setting the reconstruction adversary holds exactly such pairs, so BIE's key-secrecy assumption fails and the defense provides no measurable protection. BIE should not be added to the stack in deployments matching our threat model.

Stacking, when it does add value (e.g., dp+smpc to cover both adversary types), is not utility-costly: dp+bie and all-three both reach $0.747$ test accuracy, the highest in the matrix, because the noise-induced regularization from DP appears to absorb any optimization friction added by BIE's permuted inputs. The cost of mismatched stacking is thus wasted cryptographic effort, not utility loss.

\subsection{Limitations}
\label{sec:limitations}
There are several main limitations in our research that bound the generality of our results. First, the value of $\epsilon = 1.0$ is fixed and the $\ell_1$ sensitivity is taken as $\Delta = 1.0$ as a working assumption rather than enforced by an explicit clipping step; a sensitivity analysis (jointly over $\epsilon$ and $\Delta$) would help us better explore the tradeoff between privacy and utility and could potentially demonstrate stronger MIA resistance at lower budgets. Second, the size of our data is relatively small ($5\,856$ images) and comes from a single source; the performance of MIA attacks on other data sources might vary, especially for BIE. Third, the reconstruction baseline (no defense) sits at $19.63$ dB; a higher-PSNR baseline (e.g., above $30$ dB) would make DP's $3.2$ dB drop more visually striking. Fourth, SMPC is evaluated under the semi-honest single-corrupted-server threat model that defines its security; we do not separately report the worst-case adversary in which both servers collude or the embedding leaks post-reconstruction (in that worst case, SMPC reduces to a no-op since the head reconstructs the embedding before classification). Finally, our SMPC implementation simulates the protocol within a single process; while this faithfully captures the cryptographic guarantees (the per-server view is sampled from a distribution independent of the true embedding, as confirmed empirically by Pearson $r \approx 0.003$), a fully distributed implementation across two physical servers with Beaver-triple multiplication of additively-shared head weights is implemented in our codebase but not used in the matrix runs because the inference-time output is mathematically identical to the simulation.

\section{Conclusion}
We empirically evaluated three privacy-preserving transformations. Differential Privacy, Block-wise Image Encryption, and Secure Multi-Party Computation against membership inference and reconstruction attacks on a split-model pneumonia chest X-ray classifier across all eight on/off combinations. \emph{Each defense was evaluated under its own threat model:} an embedding-leak adversary for DP and BIE, and a single corrupted non-colluding server for SMPC.

Two defenses succeed at reconstruction, for distinct reasons. DP perturbs the embedding the adversary intercepts and reduces reconstruction PSNR by $3.2$ dB at no utility cost ($+2.2$ point accuracy gain via noise-induced regularization at $\epsilon=1.0$); it additionally produces a small reduction in Yeom MIA. SMPC provides information-theoretic independence between any single server's view and the true embedding (Pearson $r \approx 0.003$), driving single-server reconstruction PSNR to $\approx 15.5$ dB below even the no-information mean-image baseline. BIE, by contrast, fails at reconstruction in our setting because the auxiliary-pair adversary implicitly learns the permutation, and slightly amplifies Yeom MIA by inducing additional memorization on permuted inputs. SMPC is a no-op against MIA because the head reconstructs the embedding before classification.

The overarching lesson is that defense efficacy is determined by the alignment between the layer at which a mechanism intervenes and the threat model under which it is evaluated. DP and SMPC succeed because they are evaluated against the adversaries they were designed for; BIE fails because its key-secrecy assumption does not hold against an adversary with auxiliary pairs. Naively stacking mechanisms across mismatched threat models does not compound protectionit only wastes cryptographic effort. Practitioners deploying split-model medical classifiers should pick the defense whose threat model matches the deployment's actual adversary, and combine defenses only when distinct adversary types must be addressed simultaneously.
